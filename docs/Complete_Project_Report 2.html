Healthcare facilities worldwide face mounting pressure to deliver high-quality patient care while managing operational costs, regulatory compliance requirements, and increasing patient volumes. Small to medium-sized hospitals and clinics often struggle with limited IT budgets, making expensive proprietary Hospital Information Systems (HIS) financially unfeasible. The Indian healthcare sector, in particular, has witnessed rapid growth with increasing healthcare awareness, rising health insurance penetration, and government initiatives promoting digital health records. This gap in the market presents an opportunity for developing accessible, open-source or affordable hospital management solutions tailored to the specific needs of small and medium healthcare facilities. Such systems must balance comprehensive functionality with simplicity, ensuring that medical staff with varying levels of technical proficiency can efficiently utilize the platform. Additionally, the system must accommodate local requirements such as multi-currency support for international patients, integration with government health schemes, and adaptability to different specializations and hospital sizes.

From a technical perspective, advancements in web development frameworks, database technologies, cloud computing, and responsive design have made it feasible to develop sophisticated healthcare management systems using open-source technologies. 1.2 Problem Statement

Small and medium-sized hospitals and healthcare facilities face significant operational challenges stemming from the absence of integrated management systems. These challenges manifest across multiple dimensions:

Patient Management Inefficiencies: Manual patient registration processes are time-consuming, error-prone, and result in duplicate records. Physical file management systems make it difficult to quickly retrieve patient histories during emergencies. Incomplete or illegible handwritten medical records compromise care quality. The absence of centralized patient databases prevents physicians from accessing comprehensive medical histories, allergies, and treatment contraindications, increasing the risk of adverse drug reactions and medical errors.

Appointment Scheduling Conflicts: Manual appointment booking using registers or basic spreadsheets frequently results in double-booking, scheduling conflicts, and inefficient utilization of physician time. Patients experience long wait times due to poor schedule optimization. The inability to send automated appointment reminders leads to high no-show rates, wasting valuable consultation slots. Healthcare providers lack visibility into appointment patterns and physician workload distribution, making resource planning difficult.

Billing and Revenue Leakage: Manual billing processes are susceptible to calculation errors, missing charges for services rendered, and delays in generating invoices. The complexity of tracking multiple payment methods, partial payments, insurance claims, and outstanding balances manually results in revenue leakage. The absence of comprehensive financial reporting makes it challenging to identify revenue trends, monitor outstanding receivables, and make informed financial decisions. Currency conversion errors occur when serving international patients, leading to financial discrepancies.

Inventory Management Problems: Manual tracking of medical supplies, pharmaceuticals, and equipment leads to stockouts of critical items or excessive inventory carrying costs. Expired medications go unnoticed until discovered during audits, representing financial losses and potential patient safety risks. The inability to track supplier performance, item usage patterns, and reorder points results in reactive rather than proactive procurement strategies.

Communication and Coordination Gaps: Lack of integrated communication systems between departments results in delays in test results, medication administration, and patient transfers. Emergency situations require rapid access to patient information, which manual systems cannot efficiently provide. The absence of automated notifications means staff rely on verbal communication, increasing the likelihood of miscommunication and missed critical information.

Reporting and Analytics Limitations: Manual compilation of reports for regulatory compliance, performance analysis, and decision-making is labor-intensive and time-consuming. The inability to visualize trends in patient volumes, disease patterns, treatment outcomes, and financial metrics hampers strategic planning. Healthcare administrators lack data-driven insights for resource allocation, staff scheduling, and capacity planning.

1.3 Solution Overview

The Hospital Management System developed in this project provides a comprehensive, integrated solution addressing the identified challenges through a modular, web-based platform. The system architecture follows a three-tier design pattern separating presentation layer (HTML/CSS/JavaScript), business logic layer (PHP classes), and data persistence layer (SQLite database), ensuring maintainability, scalability, and separation of concerns.

Core System Capabilities: The HMS implements 18 specialized PHP classes handling distinct functional areas: Patient management (Patient.php) for complete patient lifecycle management; Doctor management (Doctor.php) for physician profiles, specializations, and schedules; Authentication and authorization (Auth.php) with role-based access control supporting five user roles (admin, doctor, nurse, receptionist, pharmacist); Appointment scheduling (Appointment.php) with conflict detection and automated reminders; Billing and payments (Billing.php, AutoBilling.php) supporting multiple currencies and payment methods; Inventory management (Inventory.php) with expiry tracking and stock alerts; Laboratory test management (Laboratory.php); Insurance claim processing (Insurance.php); Staff management (Staff.php) and scheduling (Schedule.php); Room and bed allocation (Room.php); Telemedicine capabilities (Telemedicine.php); Comprehensive reporting (Report.php, PDFReport.php); Multi-currency support (Currency.php); Data validation (Validation.php); Dashboard analytics (Dashboard.php); and AI-powered assistance (AIAssistant.php).

Database Architecture: The system utilizes a normalized SQLite database with 15 tables ensuring data integrity through foreign key relationships: users table for authentication and role management; patients table storing comprehensive patient demographics and medical information; doctors table linking physicians to user accounts with specialization details; departments table for organizational structure; appointments table tracking all scheduled consultations; medical_records table documenting diagnoses, treatments, and prescriptions; billing and billing_items tables for itemized invoice management; payments table recording transaction history; inventory table for stock management; notifications table for system alerts; ai_assistant_logs for audit trails; system_settings for configuration; and supporting tables for schedules, rooms, insurance, and laboratory tests.

User Interface Design: The frontend leverages Bootstrap 5.3.0 for responsive design ensuring consistent user experience across desktop, tablet, and mobile devices. Custom JavaScript modules (dashboard.js, patients.js, doctors.js, appointments.js, billing.js, reports.js, settings.js) provide interactive features including real-time form validation, dynamic content loading via AJAX, Chart.js visualizations for analytics, and smooth user workflows. The interface prioritizes usability for medical staff with varying technical proficiency, featuring intuitive navigation, clear information hierarchy, and minimal clicks to accomplish common tasks.

Technology Stack: PHP 8.2.12 provides the backend server-side processing with object-oriented programming principles; SQLite offers a lightweight, serverless database requiring minimal configuration; Bootstrap 5.3.0 delivers responsive CSS framework; Chart.js enables data visualization; JavaScript handles client-side interactivity; RESTful API architecture facilitates modular communication between frontend and backend; and session-based authentication ensures secure access control.

3.1 Identification of Need

The need for an integrated Hospital Management System was identified through a combination of direct observation, stakeholder interviews, and analysis of existing manual processes at several small to medium-sized healthcare facilities. The current state of operations in these facilities revealed significant inefficiencies and challenges that directly impact patient care quality, operational costs, and staff productivity.

Key Observations and Findings:

  • Fragmented Data Silos: Patient information was scattered across multiple physical registers, including registration logs, appointment diaries, billing ledgers, and individual patient files. This fragmentation made it extremely difficult to get a holistic view of a patient's medical history, leading to redundant data entry and potential for errors.
  • Manual Appointment Scheduling: Receptionists managed appointments using paper-based diaries. This process was prone to human error, such as double-booking, and offered no easy way to track doctor availability or send reminders to patients, resulting in high no-show rates and underutilized consultation slots.
  • Error-Prone Billing Processes: Billing was conducted manually, with staff calculating charges for services, consultations, and medications by hand. This led to frequent calculation errors, missed charges for services rendered (revenue leakage), and significant delays in generating final invoices, causing patient dissatisfaction.
  • Inefficient Inventory Management: Medical supplies and pharmaceuticals were tracked using basic stock registers or spreadsheets. This reactive approach provided no alerts for low stock levels or expiring items, leading to stockouts of critical supplies and financial losses from expired inventory.
  • Lack of Data for Decision-Making: Hospital administrators lacked access to real-time data on key performance indicators such as patient volume, bed occupancy rates, revenue trends, and operational costs. All reports were compiled manually, a process that was both time-consuming and often produced outdated information.

These observations clearly indicated a pressing need for a centralized, automated system that could integrate these disparate functions into a single, cohesive platform. The proposed HMS aims to digitize these workflows, providing a single source of truth for all hospital data and automating routine administrative tasks to allow medical staff to focus more on patient care.

  • The system shall provide a searchable list of all patients.

4.2 Database Design

The database is the heart of the Hospital Management System, serving as the central repository for all data. The design of the database was guided by the ER diagram (Figure 3.11) and focused on achieving normalization, ensuring data integrity, and optimizing for performance.

Schema Design: The database schema consists of 15 tables, each designed to store information about a specific entity (e.g., patients, doctors, appointments). The full schema is provided in the Appendix.

Normalization: The database was designed following the principles of normalization to reduce data redundancy and improve data integrity.

  • First Normal Form (1NF): Ensured by making sure that all table columns hold atomic values (not lists or sets) and each record is unique, which is achieved by having a primary key for every table.
  • Second Normal Form (2NF): Achieved by ensuring that all non-key attributes are fully dependent on the primary key. This is addressed by breaking down entities into separate tables. For example, patient details are in the `patients` table and appointment details are in the `appointments` table, linked by `patient_id`.
  • Third Normal Form (3NF): Achieved by removing transitive dependencies. For example, a doctor's specialization is stored in the `doctors` table, not in the `appointments` table. This prevents redundancy and update anomalies.

Data Integrity and Constraints: Several mechanisms were used to enforce data integrity at the database level:

  • Primary Keys: Each table has a primary key (usually an auto-incrementing integer `id`) to uniquely identify each record.
  • Foreign Keys: Foreign key constraints are used extensively to enforce referential integrity between tables. For example, the `appointments.patient_id` is a foreign key that references `patients.id`. This ensures that an appointment cannot be created for a non-existent patient. The `ON DELETE CASCADE` clause is used where appropriate, so that deleting a primary record (e.g., a patient) automatically deletes all related dependent records (e.g., their appointments).
  • UNIQUE Constraints: Used to ensure that values in a column or a group of columns are unique across all rows. For example, `users.username` and `patients.patient_id` have UNIQUE constraints.
  • NOT NULL Constraints: Applied to essential columns (e.g., `patients.first_name`, `appointments.appointment_date`) to ensure that no record is created without this vital information.
  • CHECK Constraints: Used to enforce domain integrity by limiting the values that can be placed in a column. For example, the `users.role` column has a CHECK constraint to ensure the value is one of the predefined roles ('admin', 'doctor', etc.).

This well-structured database design is crucial for the reliability and consistency of the data managed by the HMS.

SYSTEM SECURITY MEASURES


Security is a paramount concern for any software application, but it is especially critical for a Hospital Management System that handles sensitive Patient Health Information (PHI) and financial data. A multi-layered security approach was implemented to protect the system's confidentiality, integrity, and availability. The security measures focused on four key areas: authentication and authorization, data security, protection against common web vulnerabilities, and secure session management.

6.1 Authentication and Authorization

Authentication is the process of verifying the identity of a user, while authorization is the process of determining whether a user has permission to access a specific resource or perform a particular action.

Authentication:

  • Secure Password Storage: User passwords are never stored in plaintext. The system uses PHP's built-in `password_hash()` function to create a strong, salted hash of the user's password using the BCrypt algorithm. When a user logs in, the `password_verify()` function is used to compare the provided password against the stored hash. This makes it computationally infeasible to reverse-engineer the original password even if the database is compromised.
  • Login Mechanism: The login process requires a username and password. The system is designed to provide a generic error message ("Invalid username or password") for failed login attempts to prevent attackers from determining whether it was the username or the password that was incorrect (username enumeration).

Authorization (Role-Based Access Control - RBAC):

Once a user is authenticated, the system uses RBAC to control their access. Each user is assigned one of five roles (`admin`, `doctor`, `nurse`, `receptionist`, `pharmacist`).

  • Role Definition: Each role has a specific set of permissions. For example, only an `admin` can access the 'Settings' page, and only a `doctor` or `nurse` can update medical records.
  • Enforcement: Access control is enforced at two levels:
    1. UI Level: The navigation menu and buttons for certain actions are hidden or disabled if the user does not have the required role. For example, the 'Add New User' button is only visible to admins.
    2. API/Server Level: This is the most critical enforcement point. Every API endpoint includes a check at the beginning to verify that the logged-in user's role is authorized to perform the requested action. This prevents a user from bypassing the UI restrictions by directly calling an API endpoint. The `Auth.php` class provides a method like `checkRole(['admin', 'doctor'])` for this purpose.

6.2 Data Security

Data security involves protecting data from unauthorized access and corruption throughout its lifecycle. This includes data in transit (over the network) and data at rest (in the database).

Protection Against SQL Injection:

SQL Injection (SQLi) is a type of attack where a malicious user can interfere with the queries that an application makes to its database. The HMS prevents SQLi by exclusively using prepared statements with parameterized queries for all database interactions. The application logic uses PHP's PDO (PHP Data Objects) extension.

  • Incorrect (Vulnerable) Way: `$sql = "SELECT * FROM users WHERE username = '$username'";`
  • Correct (Secure) Way Used in HMS:
    $stmt = $this->db->prepare("SELECT * FROM users WHERE username = ?"); $stmt->execute([$username]); $user = $stmt->fetch();

By using prepared statements, the database driver is instructed to treat the user-supplied input (`$username`) as data only, not as part of the executable SQL command. This effectively neutralizes any malicious SQL code injected by an attacker.

Protection Against Cross-Site Scripting (XSS):

XSS is an attack where malicious scripts are injected into otherwise benign and trusted websites. The HMS mitigates XSS by practicing proper output encoding. Whenever user-supplied data is displayed on a web page, it is escaped to ensure that it is rendered as plain text and not interpreted as HTML or JavaScript.

  • PHP: The `htmlspecialchars()` function is used before echoing any data from the database into the HTML. For example: `<?php echo htmlspecialchars($patient['first_name']); ?>`
  • JavaScript: When dynamically adding content to the page with JavaScript, methods like `.textContent` are preferred over `.innerHTML` to prevent the browser from executing any scripts embedded in the content.

Data in Transit: While not enforced by the application code itself, it is strongly recommended that the application be deployed with HTTPS. An SSL/TLS certificate encrypts all data transmitted between the user's browser and the web server, protecting it from eavesdropping and man-in-the-middle attacks.

55

6.3 Session Management

Secure session management is crucial for maintaining the authenticated state of a user and preventing unauthorized access to their account.

Session Handling:

  • Session Initiation: A new session is created for a user only after they have been successfully authenticated. Upon login, the system calls `session_regenerate_id(true)` to generate a new, random session ID. This helps to prevent session fixation attacks, where an attacker tricks a user into using a session ID known to the attacker.
  • Session Data: Minimal, non-sensitive information is stored in the session variable (`$_SESSION`), such as `user_id`, `username`, and `role`. This data is used to identify the user on subsequent requests.
  • Session Timeout: The system is configured to automatically expire sessions after a period of inactivity (e.g., 30 minutes). This reduces the window of opportunity for an attacker to hijack an abandoned session.
  • Secure Logout: The logout functionality properly destroys the session. It unsets all session variables (`session_unset()`), destroys the session data on the server (`session_destroy()`), and clears the session cookie from the browser.

Protection Against Cross-Site Request Forgery (CSRF):

CSRF is an attack that tricks a victim into submitting a malicious request. The HMS implements the synchronizer token pattern to defend against CSRF.

  1. When a form is loaded, the server generates a unique, random token (CSRF token) and stores it in the user's session.
  2. This token is also embedded as a hidden field in the form.
  3. When the user submits the form, the server checks if the token submitted with the form matches the token stored in the session.
  4. If the tokens do not match, the request is rejected as it is likely a forged request.

This ensures that only requests originating from the application's own forms are processed, thwarting attempts by malicious websites to trick a logged-in user into performing unwanted actions.

By implementing this comprehensive set of security measures, the Hospital Management System establishes a strong defense against the most common web application threats, safeguarding the integrity of the system and the confidentiality of its data.

8.2 Challenges Faced and Solutions

No project is without its challenges. The development of the HMS encountered several technical and logistical hurdles, which provided valuable learning experiences.

Challenge 1: Preventing Appointment Conflicts

  • Problem: The initial logic for checking doctor availability was complex. It needed to account for the doctor's general schedule (e.g., Mon-Fri, 9am-5pm), existing appointments, and breaks. A simple check for an exact time match was insufficient as appointments have durations.
  • Solution: A more robust conflict detection algorithm was developed. When checking for a new appointment (e.g., 10:00 AM for 30 minutes), the query now checks for any existing appointments for that doctor that overlap with the proposed time slot [10:00 AM, 10:30 AM). This involved careful SQL querying with time-based comparisons.

Challenge 2: Handling Multiple Currencies

  • Problem: Displaying monetary values consistently across the application with the correct currency symbol and formatting was challenging. Hardcoding the currency symbol was not a scalable solution.
  • Solution: A dedicated `Currency.php` class was created. This class reads the default currency from the system settings. It provides a `format()` method that takes a numeric value and returns a string formatted with the correct currency symbol, thousands separator, and decimal points. All monetary values displayed in the UI are passed through this method, ensuring consistency and making it easy to change the currency globally.

Challenge 3: Ensuring Security Across All Endpoints

  • Problem: With numerous API endpoints, it was crucial to ensure that role-based access control was consistently applied everywhere to prevent unauthorized access. It was easy to forget to add a security check to a new endpoint.
  • Solution: A standardized structure was adopted for all API files. A central `auth_helper.php` file was created, which included a function to check the user's session and role. Every API script begins by including this helper and calling the authorization function, passing the roles allowed for that endpoint (e.g., `require_auth(['admin'])`). This made the security check a mandatory first step for every request.

Challenge 4: Generating PDF Reports

  • Problem: Generating complex, multi-page PDF reports with tables and charts from PHP can be difficult. The layout needs to be precisely controlled.
  • Solution: An external library (like FPDF or TCPDF, represented by the `PDFReport.php` class) was integrated. While this added a dependency, it abstracted away the complexities of the PDF file format. The solution involved creating a class that extended the library's base class, allowing for the creation of custom headers, footers, and table-rendering methods tailored to the HMS reports.

-- Users table for authentication CREATE TABLE users ( id INTEGER PRIMARY KEY AUTOINCREMENT, username TEXT UNIQUE NOT NULL, email TEXT UNIQUE NOT NULL, password TEXT NOT NULL, role TEXT NOT NULL CHECK (role IN ('admin', 'doctor', 'nurse', 'receptionist', 'pharmacist')), first_name TEXT NOT NULL, last_name TEXT NOT NULL, created_at DATETIME DEFAULT CURRENT_TIMESTAMP ); -- Patients table CREATE TABLE patients ( id INTEGER PRIMARY KEY AUTOINCREMENT, patient_id TEXT UNIQUE NOT NULL, first_name TEXT NOT NULL, last_name TEXT NOT NULL, date_of_birth DATE NOT NULL, gender TEXT NOT NULL, phone TEXT NOT NULL, email TEXT, address TEXT, created_at DATETIME DEFAULT CURRENT_TIMESTAMP ); -- Doctors table CREATE TABLE doctors ( id INTEGER PRIMARY KEY AUTOINCREMENT, user_id INTEGER NOT NULL, specialization TEXT NOT NULL, consultation_fee REAL, FOREIGN KEY (user_id) REFERENCES users(id) ON DELETE CASCADE ); -- Appointments table CREATE TABLE appointments ( id INTEGER PRIMARY KEY AUTOINCREMENT, patient_id INTEGER NOT NULL, doctor_id INTEGER NOT NULL, appointment_date DATE NOT NULL, appointment_time TEXT NOT NULL, status TEXT DEFAULT 'scheduled' CHECK (status IN ('scheduled', 'completed', 'cancelled')), FOREIGN KEY (patient_id) REFERENCES patients(id) ON DELETE CASCADE, FOREIGN KEY (doctor_id) REFERENCES doctors(id) ON DELETE CASCADE ); -- ...
